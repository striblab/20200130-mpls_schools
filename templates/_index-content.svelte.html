How Minneapolis might reshape its school district

By Faiza Mahamud, MaryJo Webster and Jeff Hargarten
Star Tribune

February 7, 2020

Minneapolis Public Schools recently unveiled its final proposed plan, which called for sweeping changes that district leaders say will dismantle disparities and help close the achievement gap between white students and students of color.

In September 2019, Ed Graff announced a new timeline for his restructuring plan. The district will continue with community engagement until April, when the school board is expected to take a vote. Structural changes would not happen until the 2021-22 school year, with some of the bigger changes taking longer to implement.

The district has been operating under the current plan for more than six years and has since shrunk in size to 33,380. Now, the district is facing a $19 million budget shortfall for the next school year thanks in large part to a loss of enrollment over the past year.

There’s an increased urgency among district leaders to mitigate this problem and save a once coveted school system and the largest in the state from collapsing.

How magnet schools might shift

The district currently has about 14 magnet schools and spends about $4.6 million transporting students to those schools, which are mostly situated in the southern part of the city. District data shows that the current structure has led to more segregated schools and a growing academic disparities. Meanwhile, schools in north and northeast Minneapolis have fallen victim to the district’s current choice policy, making those schools less attractive to its residents.
